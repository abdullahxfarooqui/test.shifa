# Heart Attack Warning Signs: What to Do in the First 10 Minutes

A heart attack occurs when blood flow to part of the heart muscle is blocked, usually by a clot. Every minute without treatment destroys more heart muscle — which is why recognizing the warning signs and acting within the first 10 minutes dramatically improves survival and recovery.

## The 7 Warning Signs of a Heart Attack

Most people picture a dramatic collapse, but heart attacks often present subtly — especially in women and people with diabetes. Know these warning signs:

### 1. Chest Pain or Pressure
The most common symptom is a feeling of tightness, squeezing, heaviness, or pressure in the centre of the chest. It may come and go or persist for more than a few minutes. Do not wait to see if it passes.

### 2. Pain Spreading to the Arm, Neck, or Jaw
Discomfort that radiates to the left arm, both arms, the neck, jaw, or back is a classic cardiac warning. This referred pain occurs because the heart shares nerve pathways with these regions.

### 3. Shortness of Breath
Sudden difficulty breathing — with or without chest discomfort — signals that the heart is struggling to pump oxygenated blood. This can appear before chest pain begins.

### 4. Cold Sweat
Breaking into a sudden, cold sweat for no obvious reason (not heat, not exertion) is a recognised warning sign. The body activates its stress response when the heart is under threat.

### 5. Nausea or Vomiting
Particularly common in women, nausea that comes on suddenly without a gastrointestinal cause warrants immediate attention alongside other symptoms.

### 6. Lightheadedness or Dizziness
Feeling faint or dizzy — especially combined with chest discomfort or shortness of breath — can indicate reduced cardiac output.

### 7. Unexplained Fatigue
Extreme, sudden fatigue — feeling as though even small movements are exhausting — can precede a heart attack by hours or days, particularly in women.

## What to Do in the First 10 Minutes

**Step 1 — Call emergency services immediately (1122 / 115).** Do not drive yourself to hospital. Emergency responders can begin treatment before arrival.

**Step 2 — Chew (do not swallow whole) a 300mg aspirin tablet** if you are not allergic and have no recent history of stomach bleeding. Aspirin inhibits further clotting.

**Step 3 — Sit or lie down** in a position that reduces strain. Loosen any tight clothing.

**Step 4 — Unlock your front door** so paramedics can enter immediately if you lose consciousness.

**Step 5 — Stay on the line** with the emergency operator. They will guide you through exactly what to do.

Do not eat or drink anything else. Do not take nitroglycerin unless prescribed. Do not wait to see if symptoms improve — every minute counts.

## Who Is at Risk?

Risk factors for heart attack include:

- High blood pressure (hypertension)
- High LDL cholesterol or low HDL cholesterol
- Type 2 diabetes or insulin resistance
- Smoking — even secondhand smoke
- Obesity, particularly abdominal obesity
- Physical inactivity
- Family history of heart disease
- Chronic stress and poor sleep
- Age over 45 in men, post-menopause in women

In Pakistan, heart disease is the leading cause of death. Urban populations in Islamabad and Karachi show rising rates due to sedentary lifestyles, high-sodium diets, and increasing rates of diabetes.

## Heart Attack vs. Cardiac Arrest: Key Difference

A heart attack is a circulation problem — a blocked artery. The heart continues beating, but part of the muscle is dying. A cardiac arrest is an electrical problem — the heart stops beating entirely. Cardiac arrest can follow a heart attack but is a separate emergency requiring immediate CPR.

If someone collapses and is unresponsive with no normal breathing, begin CPR at 100–120 compressions per minute and call emergency services.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can a heart attack happen without chest pain?**
Yes. Silent heart attacks — presenting with fatigue, shortness of breath, or jaw pain but no chest pain — account for roughly 45% of all heart attacks and are more common in women, elderly individuals, and those with diabetes.

**Q: How long does a heart attack last?**
Symptoms can last from a few minutes to several hours. Even if discomfort subsides, the underlying blockage may persist. Always seek emergency care regardless of whether symptoms improve.

**Q: Is jaw pain a sign of a heart attack?**
Yes. Pain or tightness in the jaw, particularly the lower jaw, can be referred pain from cardiac ischaemia. When accompanied by chest pressure, shortness of breath, or sweating, treat it as a cardiac emergency.

**Q: Can young people have heart attacks?**
Yes. While risk increases with age, heart attacks can occur in people in their 20s and 30s, particularly with strong family history, cocaine use, uncontrolled hypertension, or undiagnosed cholesterol disorders.

**Q: What is the difference between a heart attack and angina?**
Angina is chest pain caused by reduced blood flow to the heart during exertion or stress. It typically resolves with rest or nitroglycerin in under 15 minutes. A heart attack occurs when a coronary artery is completely blocked and symptoms persist at rest. Angina that changes character, worsens at rest, or lasts longer than usual (unstable angina) requires emergency evaluation.

# Type 2 Diabetes: Prevention and Management in Pakistan

Pakistan has the third-highest number of people living with diabetes in the world — over 33 million adults. Type 2 diabetes is preventable in many cases and manageable in all cases. Understanding the condition is the first step toward protecting your health.

## What Is Type 2 Diabetes?

Type 2 diabetes is a chronic metabolic condition in which the body either does not produce enough insulin or cannot effectively use the insulin it produces. The result: blood glucose levels rise higher than normal (hyperglycaemia), damaging blood vessels and nerves throughout the body over time.

Unlike type 1 diabetes — an autoimmune condition — type 2 diabetes is strongly influenced by lifestyle, though genetics also play a significant role.

## Symptoms of Type 2 Diabetes

Many people have type 2 diabetes for years without knowing it. When symptoms do appear, they include:

- Increased thirst and frequent urination
- Unexplained fatigue, particularly after meals
- Blurred vision
- Slow-healing cuts or wounds
- Frequent infections (skin, gum, or urinary tract)
- Numbness or tingling in the hands or feet
- Darkened skin in skin folds (acanthosis nigricans) — a sign of insulin resistance

If you have any of these symptoms, request a fasting blood glucose test from your doctor.

## Diagnosing Diabetes: What the Numbers Mean

| Test | Normal | Pre-diabetes | Diabetes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fasting blood glucose | < 5.6 mmol/L (< 100 mg/dL) | 5.6–6.9 mmol/L (100–125 mg/dL) | ≥ 7.0 mmol/L (≥ 126 mg/dL) |
| HbA1c | < 5.7% | 5.7–6.4% | ≥ 6.5% |
| 2-hour glucose (OGTT) | < 7.8 mmol/L | 7.8–11.0 mmol/L | ≥ 11.1 mmol/L |

Pre-diabetes is a critical intervention window. With the right changes, progression to type 2 diabetes can be delayed or prevented entirely.

## Risk Factors Specific to Pakistan

Several factors contribute to Pakistan's high diabetes prevalence:

- **Genetics**: South Asians develop type 2 diabetes at lower BMI thresholds than Western populations — often at a BMI of 23–25 kg/m² rather than the conventional ≥ 30 kg/m²
- **Diet**: High consumption of refined carbohydrates (white rice, naan, white bread, sugary chai), and processed foods
- **Physical inactivity**: Urban, sedentary lifestyles; low rates of structured exercise
- **Abdominal obesity**: Central fat accumulation drives insulin resistance
- **Stress and poor sleep**: Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which raises blood glucose
- **Low awareness**: Many diagnoses are made only when complications appear

## How to Prevent Type 2 Diabetes

Evidence from the Diabetes Prevention Program and similar trials shows that structured lifestyle change reduces diabetes risk by 58% — more effective than medication alone.

### Diet Changes That Work

- Replace refined grains with whole grains (brown rice, whole wheat, oats, barley)
- Reduce portion sizes — use a smaller plate
- Eat vegetables at every meal, especially non-starchy vegetables
- Limit sugary beverages including packaged juices and sweetened chai
- Include protein (eggs, daal, chicken, fish) with each meal to slow glucose absorption
- Avoid late-night eating — insulin sensitivity is lowest at night

### Physical Activity

- Aim for 150 minutes of moderate-intensity activity per week (brisk walking, cycling, swimming)
- Even 10-minute walks after each meal meaningfully reduce post-meal blood glucose spikes
- Resistance training (bodyweight exercises, weights) improves insulin sensitivity

### Weight Management

Losing 5–10% of body weight reduces diabetes risk significantly. In someone weighing 85 kg, that is just 4–8 kg.

## Managing Type 2 Diabetes

For those already diagnosed, the goal is maintaining blood glucose within target range to prevent complications.

### Blood Glucose Targets (HbA1c)

- General target: < 7.0%
- Older adults or those with significant comorbidities: < 8.0%
- Young, otherwise healthy adults: < 6.5%

### Medications

Your doctor may prescribe one or more of the following depending on your HbA1c, kidney function, and cardiovascular risk:

- **Metformin** — first-line medication; reduces liver glucose output
- **SGLT-2 inhibitors** (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) — reduce cardiovascular risk, protect kidneys
- **GLP-1 receptor agonists** (semaglutide, liraglutide) — promote weight loss, reduce cardiovascular events
- **Sulfonylureas** (glibenclamide, glimepiride) — lower cost, may cause hypoglycaemia
- **Insulin** — required when other medications are insufficient

Never stop or adjust diabetes medication without consulting your doctor.

### Monitoring at Home

- Check fasting blood glucose on waking (target: 4.0–7.0 mmol/L / 72–126 mg/dL)
- Check 2-hour post-meal glucose (target: < 10.0 mmol/L / < 180 mg/dL)
- Get HbA1c checked every 3 months until stable, then every 6 months

### Annual Checks

Diabetes can silently damage kidneys, eyes, nerves, and blood vessels. Every year, get:

- Kidney function (eGFR, urine microalbumin)
- Eye examination (dilated fundoscopy)
- Foot examination
- Lipid panel and blood pressure check
- Dental check (gum disease worsens blood sugar control)

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can type 2 diabetes be reversed?**
Type 2 diabetes can go into remission — defined as HbA1c below 6.5% without medication — through significant weight loss (typically 10–15% of body weight), very low calorie diets, or bariatric surgery. Remission is not guaranteed for everyone but is achievable, particularly in those recently diagnosed.

**Q: What is a normal blood sugar level after eating in Pakistan?**
Two hours after a meal, a normal blood glucose level is below 7.8 mmol/L (140 mg/dL). Between 7.8–11.0 mmol/L indicates pre-diabetes. Above 11.1 mmol/L is consistent with diabetes.

**Q: Is chai (tea) bad for diabetes?**
Plain unsweetened chai with minimal milk is acceptable. However, the typical Pakistani chai preparation — with 2–4 teaspoons of sugar per cup, drunk 3–5 times daily — delivers significant sugar load. Switching to unsweetened chai or reducing sugar gradually is one of the most impactful dietary changes for blood sugar control.

**Q: How often should a diabetic check blood sugar?**
People on insulin should check at least 2–4 times daily (before meals and at bedtime). Those on oral medication should check fasting blood glucose daily and 2-hour post-meal glucose 2–3 times per week, or more frequently when medication is being adjusted.

**Q: What foods should a diabetic avoid in Pakistan?**
Prioritise reducing: white rice (especially large portions), naan, packaged biscuits and crackers, sugary beverages, fruit juices, mithai, and deep-fried foods. These are not permanently off-limits but should be consumed in small quantities and paired with protein or fibre to blunt the glucose spike.

# PCOS: Causes, Symptoms, and Treatment Options for Pakistani Women

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) affects approximately 1 in 5 women of reproductive age in Pakistan — making it one of the most common hormonal disorders among women. Despite its prevalence, it is frequently misdiagnosed or dismissed. This guide explains what PCOS is, how to recognise it, and what treatment options are available.

## What Is PCOS?

PCOS is a hormonal disorder characterised by elevated androgens (male hormones), irregular or absent menstrual cycles, and — in many women — multiple small follicles on the ovaries visible on ultrasound. The name is slightly misleading: the ovaries do not contain true cysts but rather many small, underdeveloped follicles.

PCOS is also closely linked to insulin resistance, which means the body does not respond efficiently to insulin and must produce more of it. Excess insulin stimulates the ovaries to produce more androgens, which disrupts ovulation.

## Causes of PCOS

No single cause has been identified. PCOS is believed to result from a combination of:

- **Genetics**: PCOS runs in families. Having a mother or sister with PCOS significantly increases your risk
- **Insulin resistance**: Present in up to 70% of women with PCOS, regardless of weight
- **Inflammation**: Low-grade chronic inflammation appears to stimulate androgen production
- **Hormonal imbalance**: Elevated LH (luteinising hormone) relative to FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone) disrupts the normal cycle of follicle development

## Symptoms of PCOS

Symptoms vary considerably between women. The most common include:

### Menstrual Irregularities
- Periods that come fewer than 8 times per year, or not at all
- Very light or very heavy periods
- Cycles longer than 35 days

### Signs of High Androgens
- Excess facial or body hair (hirsutism), particularly on the chin, upper lip, chest, or abdomen
- Acne on the face, chest, or back — often cystic, hormonal in pattern
- Thinning scalp hair or male-pattern hair loss

### Metabolic Symptoms
- Weight gain, particularly around the abdomen — or difficulty losing weight
- Skin darkening in the armpits, groin, or neck (acanthosis nigricans) — a sign of insulin resistance
- Skin tags

### Other Symptoms
- Mood changes, anxiety, and depression — significantly more common in women with PCOS
- Sleep apnoea — particularly in those who are overweight
- Difficulty conceiving — PCOS is the most common cause of anovulatory infertility

## Diagnosing PCOS

The Rotterdam criteria require at least 2 of the following 3 features:

1. Irregular or absent ovulation (evidenced by irregular periods)
2. Clinical or biochemical signs of high androgens
3. Polycystic ovaries on ultrasound (12 or more follicles 2–9mm in diameter in either ovary, or ovarian volume > 10 mL)

Other conditions must first be ruled out, including thyroid disorders, hyperprolactinaemia, congenital adrenal hyperplasia, and Cushing's syndrome.

Your gynaecologist will typically request:
- Day 2–5 hormone panel: LH, FSH, oestradiol, testosterone, prolactin, TSH
- Fasting blood glucose, fasting insulin, and HbA1c
- Pelvic ultrasound
- Lipid panel (PCOS increases cardiovascular risk)

## Treatment Options

PCOS is a lifelong condition without a cure, but its symptoms are manageable with the right combination of lifestyle and medical treatment.

### Lifestyle Changes (First-Line Treatment)

Weight loss of 5–10% in women who are overweight reduces androgen levels, restores ovulation in many cases, and improves insulin sensitivity. Even modest changes produce significant hormonal improvement.

**Diet**: A low-glycaemic diet (whole grains, legumes, vegetables, lean protein) reduces insulin spikes. Mediterranean-style eating patterns have the strongest evidence. Reduce refined carbohydrates, sugary foods, and packaged snacks.

**Exercise**: 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity exercise reduces insulin resistance independently of weight loss. A combination of aerobic and resistance training is most effective.

**Sleep**: Poor sleep worsens insulin resistance and cortisol levels. Prioritise 7–9 hours.

### Medications for Menstrual Regulation
- **Combined oral contraceptive pill (OCP)**: Regulates cycles, reduces androgen levels, clears hormonal acne, and manages hirsutism. Not suitable for women trying to conceive
- **Cyclical progestogens**: Induce withdrawal bleeds and protect the uterine lining when OCPs are contraindicated

### Medications for Insulin Resistance
- **Metformin**: Improves insulin sensitivity, lowers androgen levels, and can restore ovulation. Particularly useful in women with pre-diabetes or metabolic PCOS phenotype

### Medications for Fertility
- **Letrozole (first-line)**: An aromatase inhibitor that stimulates ovulation. Achieves higher live birth rates than clomiphene in PCOS
- **Clomiphene citrate**: Ovulation induction; older first-line agent now largely replaced by letrozole
- **Gonadotropin injections**: Used in women who do not respond to oral ovulation induction
- **IVF with IVM (in vitro maturation)**: For women who do not conceive with ovulation induction

### Medications for Hirsutism and Acne
- **Anti-androgens** (spironolactone, cyproterone acetate): Reduce hair growth and acne but require contraception as they are teratogenic
- **Eflornithine cream**: Slows facial hair growth
- **Laser hair removal**: Effective long-term solution for facial and body hair

## Long-Term Health Risks

Women with untreated PCOS have higher lifetime risk of:

- Type 2 diabetes (up to 10 times higher risk)
- Cardiovascular disease
- Endometrial cancer (due to unopposed oestrogen from anovulation)
- Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease
- Depression and anxiety

Regular monitoring — fasting glucose, lipid panel, blood pressure, and menstrual tracking — allows early intervention.

---

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can PCOS be cured permanently?**
PCOS cannot be permanently cured, but symptoms can be effectively managed throughout life. For some women, symptoms improve after menopause as androgen levels naturally decline.

**Q: Can a woman with PCOS get pregnant naturally?**
Yes. Many women with PCOS conceive naturally, particularly with lifestyle changes that restore regular ovulation. For those who do not, ovulation-induction medications (letrozole) are effective first-line fertility treatment.

**Q: Does PCOS always cause weight gain?**
No. Approximately 20–30% of women with PCOS are of normal weight or lean. These women still have insulin resistance and hormonal imbalance and are equally at risk of complications.

**Q: Is irregular periods always PCOS?**
Not necessarily. Irregular periods have many causes including thyroid disorders, hyperprolactinaemia, premature ovarian insufficiency, eating disorders, extreme exercise, and stress. A proper hormonal evaluation is needed to confirm PCOS and rule out other causes.

**Q: What tests confirm PCOS?**
Diagnosis requires clinical evaluation, a hormone blood panel (LH, FSH, testosterone, prolactin, TSH), and pelvic ultrasound. No single test confirms PCOS — it is a diagnosis of exclusion combined with clinical criteria.

# Stroke Warning Signs: How to Recognize a Brain Attack and Act FAST

A stroke occurs every 40 seconds worldwide. In Pakistan, stroke is a leading cause of adult disability and death — yet up to 80% of strokes are preventable. Recognising the warning signs and acting within the first hour dramatically changes outcomes. Time lost is brain lost.

## What Is a Stroke?

A stroke is a medical emergency that occurs when blood supply to part of the brain is cut off. Without blood, brain cells begin dying within minutes.

There are two main types:

- **Ischaemic stroke (85% of cases)**: A blood clot blocks a cerebral artery. Caused by artery-to-artery embolism, cardiac embolism (e.g., from atrial fibrillation), or small vessel disease
- **Haemorrhagic stroke (15%)**: A blood vessel ruptures, causing bleeding into or around the brain. Often related to uncontrolled hypertension

A **TIA (transient ischaemic attack)** — sometimes called a mini-stroke — produces identical symptoms that resolve within 24 hours. TIAs are medical emergencies: 1 in 10 people have a full stroke within 48 hours of a TIA.

## The FAST Test: Recognize a Stroke in Seconds

**F — Face drooping**
Ask the person to smile. Is one side of the face drooping or numb? An uneven smile is a warning sign.

**A — Arm weakness**
Ask them to raise both arms. Does one arm drift downward or feel weak?

**S — Speech difficulty**
Ask them to repeat a simple sentence ("The sky is blue"). Is speech slurred, garbled, or impossible?

**T — Time to call emergency services**
If any of these signs are present, call 1122 or 115 immediately. Note the exact time symptoms began — this determines treatment eligibility.

### Additional Warning Signs

Beyond FAST, watch for sudden onset of:

- Severe headache with no known cause — described as "the worst headache of my life" (suggests haemorrhagic stroke)
- Vision disturbance in one or both eyes (blurring, double vision, loss of vision)
- Dizziness, loss of balance, or sudden falls
- Confusion or difficulty understanding others
- Numbness or weakness on one side of the body (face, arm, or leg)

## Why the First Hour Is Critical

For ischaemic stroke, the clot-busting medication **tPA (tissue plasminogen activator)** can be given intravenously within 4.5 hours of symptom onset — but its effectiveness is dramatically greater in the first 60–90 minutes. Beyond 4.5 hours, it is no longer beneficial.

**Mechanical thrombectomy** — using a catheter to physically retrieve the clot from a brain artery — can be performed up to 24 hours after onset in selected patients.

For haemorrhagic stroke, surgery or minimally invasive drainage may be required to relieve pressure on the brain.

Every 15-minute reduction in time to treatment prevents thousands of additional brain cells from dying.

## Risk Factors for Stroke

### Controllable Risk Factors
- **Hypertension** — the single most important modifiable risk factor; responsible for up to 50% of strokes
- **Atrial fibrillation** — irregular heart rhythm that causes clots to form in the heart and travel to the brain
- **Diabetes** — doubles stroke risk
- **High cholesterol** — contributes to arterial plaque
- **Smoking** — doubles stroke risk; cessation reduces risk within years
- **Obesity and physical inactivity**
- **Excessive alcohol consumption**
- **Uncontrolled sleep apnoea**

### Non-Modifiable Risk Factors
- Age (risk doubles every decade after 55)
- Male sex (men have higher stroke rates; women have worse outcomes)
- Family history of stroke
- Prior stroke or TIA

## Stroke Rehabilitation

Recovery after stroke depends on the extent of brain damage and how quickly treatment was given. Rehabilitation begins within 24–48 hours of a stable stroke and may include:

- **Physiotherapy**: Rebuilding movement and balance
- **Occupational therapy**: Relearning daily activities
- **Speech and language therapy**: Addressing aphasia and swallowing difficulties
- **Neuropsychology**: Managing cognitive and emotional changes
- **Social support**: Depression affects up to 30% of stroke survivors

Recovery can continue for months to years with structured rehabilitation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What does a stroke feel like?**
Stroke symptoms come on suddenly. Common descriptions include sudden weakness or numbness on one side of the body, sudden inability to speak or understand speech, sudden vision loss in one eye, or a sudden severe headache unlike any previous headache. Stroke is rarely painful — the brain has no pain receptors.

**Q: Can a young person have a stroke?**
Yes. Approximately 10–15% of strokes occur in people under 45. Causes in younger adults include patent foramen ovale (a hole in the heart), arterial dissection, hypercoagulable states, vasculitis, and illicit drug use.

**Q: How is a stroke different from a migraine?**
Both can cause headache, visual disturbance, and neurological symptoms. Key differences: migraine symptoms typically spread gradually over minutes (march), whereas stroke symptoms are sudden and maximal at onset. Migraine aura typically causes positive symptoms (flashing lights, zigzag patterns); stroke typically causes negative symptoms (loss of vision, weakness, numbness).

**Q: Can you have a stroke without knowing it?**
Yes. Silent strokes occur without obvious symptoms and are discovered incidentally on brain MRI. They increase the risk of future symptomatic strokes and cognitive decline.

**Q: What is the best way to prevent a stroke?**
Control blood pressure (target < 130/80 mmHg), take anticoagulants if prescribed for atrial fibrillation, manage cholesterol and blood glucose, quit smoking, exercise regularly, limit alcohol, and treat sleep apnoea. For those with prior TIA or stroke, antiplatelet medications (aspirin, clopidogrel) or anticoagulation significantly reduce recurrence risk.

# Knee Pain: Causes, Red Flags, and When to See a Doctor

Knee pain is among the most common musculoskeletal complaints seen at Shifa Orthopaedic Centre. It affects people of all ages — from young athletes with ligament injuries to older adults with osteoarthritis. Understanding the cause guides the right treatment and prevents permanent joint damage.

## Common Causes of Knee Pain

### Osteoarthritis
The most common cause in adults over 40. Cartilage — the smooth tissue covering the ends of bones — gradually wears away, causing pain, stiffness, and swelling. Symptoms typically worsen with activity and improve with rest early in the disease, but progress to constant pain in advanced stages.

### Meniscus Tears
The menisci are C-shaped cartilage discs that cushion the knee joint. Tears occur acutely with twisting injuries or gradually from repetitive wear. Symptoms include pain along the joint line, swelling, locking or clicking of the knee, and difficulty fully straightening or bending.

### ACL (Anterior Cruciate Ligament) Tears
A common sports injury caused by sudden stopping, landing, or pivoting. Often accompanied by a "pop" sound, rapid swelling within hours (haemarthrosis), instability (the knee "gives way"), and inability to bear weight. ACL tears are a surgical emergency in active individuals.

### Patellofemoral Pain Syndrome (Runner's Knee)
Diffuse pain around or behind the kneecap, worsened by climbing stairs, kneeling, squatting, or sitting with knees bent for prolonged periods. Common in runners, cyclists, and young women. Often responds well to physiotherapy.

### Patellar Tendinopathy (Jumper's Knee)
Pain at the base of the kneecap caused by overuse or repetitive jumping. Common in basketball and volleyball players. Characterised by a warm, tender lump at the tendon attachment.

### Bursitis
Inflammation of the bursa sacs that cushion the knee. Causes localised swelling, warmth, and tenderness — often directly over the kneecap (prepatellar bursitis) or on the inner side of the knee.

### Gout and Pseudogout
Crystals depositing in the joint cause acute, severe, and rapid-onset pain, redness, and swelling. Gout involves uric acid crystals; pseudogout involves calcium pyrophosphate. Both require blood tests and joint fluid analysis to confirm.

## Red Flag Symptoms: Seek Urgent Care

Go to the emergency department or call for immediate evaluation if you experience:

- Knee pain following significant trauma (fall, road traffic accident)
- Complete inability to bear weight
- Visible deformity of the knee or leg
- Severe swelling that develops rapidly (within 2 hours of injury)
- Fever alongside knee pain and swelling (suggests septic arthritis — a joint infection requiring emergency drainage)
- Locking of the knee in a fixed position

## When to Book an Appointment (Non-Urgent)

Schedule an orthopaedic evaluation when knee pain:

- Persists for more than 2 weeks without improvement
- Interferes with daily activities (walking, climbing stairs, sleep)
- Is worsening despite rest and over-the-counter pain relief
- Recurs frequently or is associated with swelling
- Is accompanied by instability, clicking, catching, or giving-way

## Diagnosis: What to Expect

Your orthopaedic surgeon will conduct a thorough history and physical examination. They may request:

- **X-ray**: Evaluates bone alignment, joint space narrowing (osteoarthritis), and fractures
- **MRI**: The gold standard for soft tissue evaluation — menisci, ligaments, cartilage
- **Ultrasound**: Useful for assessing tendons and bursae
- **Blood tests**: Uric acid (gout), inflammatory markers (rheumatoid arthritis), blood count
- **Joint aspiration**: Fluid is withdrawn and sent for analysis when infection or crystal disease is suspected

## Treatment Options

### Conservative (Non-Surgical) Treatment

Most knee pain responds to non-surgical management:

- **RICE protocol** (for acute injuries): Rest, Ice, Compression, Elevation for the first 48–72 hours
- **Physiotherapy**: Strengthening quadriceps and hamstrings reduces load on the joint and improves stability
- **Weight management**: Every 1 kg of body weight lost reduces knee joint load by approximately 4 kg
- **Oral anti-inflammatories**: NSAIDs (ibuprofen, diclofenac) for short-term pain and inflammation relief
- **Intra-articular injections**: Corticosteroid injections reduce inflammation; hyaluronic acid injections lubricate the joint
- **PRP (platelet-rich plasma) therapy**: Emerging evidence for early osteoarthritis and tendon injuries

### Surgical Treatment

Surgery is considered when conservative treatment fails or when the injury cannot heal without it:

- **Arthroscopic meniscus repair or partial meniscectomy**: Keyhole surgery for meniscus tears
- **ACL reconstruction**: Using a tendon graft to replace the torn ligament; returns most patients to sport within 9–12 months
- **Total knee replacement (TKR)**: Reserved for severe, end-stage osteoarthritis with significant functional impairment; produces reliable long-term pain relief and improved quality of life
- **Unicompartmental knee replacement**: Resurfacing only the affected compartment; shorter recovery, appropriate in selected patients

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can knee pain go away on its own?**
Minor knee pain from muscle strain or minor overuse often resolves within 2 weeks with rest, ice, and activity modification. Pain from structural damage — ligament tears, meniscus tears, or advanced osteoarthritis — does not resolve on its own and worsens without appropriate treatment.

**Q: Is cracking or popping in the knee normal?**
Painless clicking or popping is common and usually benign (caused by gas bubbles or tendons snapping over bony surfaces). Clicking accompanied by pain, swelling, or instability warrants evaluation for meniscal or cartilage damage.

**Q: At what point is knee replacement recommended?**
Knee replacement is recommended when severe osteoarthritis causes persistent pain that significantly limits daily activities, and when 6 or more months of conservative treatment (physiotherapy, injections, weight management) has not provided adequate relief. Age alone is not a contraindication.

**Q: How long is recovery from knee replacement surgery?**
Most patients walk with a frame within 24 hours of surgery. Hospital stay is typically 3–5 days. Patients return to light daily activities within 6 weeks and full recovery, including stair climbing and longer walks, within 3–6 months.

**Q: What exercises are safe with knee osteoarthritis?**
Low-impact exercise is recommended: swimming, water aerobics, cycling, and walking on flat surfaces. Avoid high-impact activities (running on hard surfaces, jumping). Straight-leg raises, quadriceps sets, and inner range quads exercises can be done without aggravating the joint.

## Common Parent Concerns

### "Will multiple vaccines at one visit overwhelm my baby's immune system?"

No. A healthy infant's immune system can safely respond to thousands of antigens simultaneously. Giving multiple vaccines at one visit reduces the number of clinic visits, reduces discomfort overall, and ensures protection is in place earlier. The immune burden from the combined EPI schedule is a tiny fraction of what the immune system handles every day.

### "Are vaccine side effects dangerous?"

Most side effects are mild and expected: redness or tenderness at the injection site, low-grade fever (up to 38.5°C), and fussiness for 1–2 days. These are signs the immune system is building protection, not signs of illness.

Serious allergic reactions (anaphylaxis) are extremely rare — approximately 1 in 1 million doses — and occur within 15 minutes of vaccination. This is why your child is observed for 15 minutes after each vaccine.

If fever exceeds 39°C, the site swelling extends beyond the joint, or your child seems excessively sleepy or inconsolable, contact your paediatrician.

### "My child missed their scheduled vaccines. Is it too late?"

It is never too late to vaccinate. Pakistan's EPI and Shifa's vaccine clinic offer catch-up vaccination for children and adolescents of any age. Your paediatrician will create a catch-up schedule to get your child protected as quickly as possible.

### "Can my child be vaccinated if they have a cold?"

Yes. A mild illness (runny nose, low-grade fever, mild cough) is not a reason to postpone vaccination. Only moderate-to-severe illness warrants delay. Postponing vaccines due to mild illness leaves children unprotected and leads to missed appointments.

- **Japanese Encephalitis**: For travel to South-East Asia and rural Far East
- **Rabies pre-exposure prophylaxis**: For travel to areas with high dog rabies and limited post-exposure access
- **Typhoid** (enhanced): Injectable typhoid conjugate vaccine preferred over oral for travel to high-risk areas

Consult Shifa's travel medicine clinic at least 4–6 weeks before travel.

**Q: What vaccines are mandatory for school admission in Pakistan?**

# Depression in Pakistan: Signs, Causes, and How to Get Help

Depression is not sadness. It is not weakness, laziness, or a lack of faith. Clinical depression is a medical condition — as real as diabetes or hypertension — that affects brain chemistry, thought patterns, and physical health. In Pakistan, depression affects an estimated 34% of women and 21% of men at some point in their lives, yet fewer than 10% receive appropriate treatment. This guide explains what depression is, how to recognise it, and how to get help.

## What Is Clinical Depression?

Clinical depression (major depressive disorder) is a persistent condition characterised by low mood, loss of interest or pleasure, and a range of physical and cognitive symptoms lasting at least two weeks. It is distinct from normal sadness, grief, or temporary low mood — which are appropriate responses to difficult circumstances and typically resolve with time.

Depression changes brain chemistry. Neurotransmitter systems (serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine), the stress-hormone axis (HPA axis), and brain structure itself are all affected. This is why willpower alone is insufficient treatment.

## Recognising Depression: The 9 Diagnostic Symptoms

Depression is diagnosed when at least 5 of the following 9 symptoms are present for at least two weeks, and at least one is either depressed mood or loss of interest:

1. **Depressed mood** most of the day, nearly every day (feels sad, empty, hopeless; may feel like crying for no reason)
2. **Markedly diminished interest or pleasure** in all, or almost all, activities (anhedonia — things that used to bring joy no longer do)
3. **Significant weight change** — either loss of appetite and weight, or increased appetite and weight gain
4. **Sleep disturbance** — either inability to sleep (insomnia) or sleeping too much (hypersomnia)
5. **Psychomotor changes** — feeling slowed down in movement and thought (psychomotor retardation) or feeling agitated and restless
6. **Fatigue or loss of energy** nearly every day
7. **Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt** that are not proportionate to circumstances
8. **Difficulty concentrating, thinking clearly, or making decisions**
9. **Recurrent thoughts of death or suicide**, or a specific plan or attempt

## How Depression Presents Differently in Pakistan

In many South Asian cultures, depression often presents somatically — through physical symptoms rather than emotional ones. Common presentations include:

- Persistent headache, back pain, or body aches without clear physical cause
- Chronic fatigue that does not improve with rest
- Digestive problems (nausea, loss of appetite)
- Palpitations or chest tightness
- "My heart is heavy" or "my mind does not function"

These physical complaints are genuine — depression dysregulates the autonomic nervous system and pain pathways — but are often attributed to other causes, delaying correct diagnosis.

## What Causes Depression?

Depression is caused by a combination of:

- **Biological factors**: Genetic predisposition (family history of depression doubles the risk), dysregulation of neurotransmitter systems, hormonal changes (postpartum, thyroid, menopause)
- **Psychological factors**: Negative thinking patterns, perfectionism, low self-worth, history of trauma or abuse
- **Social and environmental factors**: Poverty, unemployment, domestic conflict, social isolation, chronic illness, bereavement, major life stressors

Depression is not caused by personal weakness, lack of religious devotion, or being ungrateful. These beliefs prevent people from seeking care and worsen outcomes.

## Treatment Options

Depression is highly treatable. Between 70–90% of people respond to treatment. The two main evidence-based approaches are psychotherapy and medication, and combining both is more effective than either alone.

### Psychotherapy

- **Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)**: The most extensively researched therapy for depression. Identifies and challenges distorted thinking patterns that maintain low mood. Typically 12–20 sessions
- **Interpersonal Therapy (IPT)**: Focuses on relationship difficulties and life transitions that contribute to depression. Effective in 12–16 sessions
- **Behavioural Activation**: Structured gradual re-engagement with rewarding activities. Particularly useful early in treatment when motivation is lowest

Shifa's psychiatry department provides both individual and group therapy in Urdu and English.

### Antidepressant Medications

Antidepressants are not addictive and do not change your personality. They restore neurochemical balance to allow the therapy process — and your own coping — to be effective.

- **SSRIs (selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors)** — first-line: fluoxetine, sertraline, escitalopram. Taken daily, effects typically begin within 2–4 weeks, full response by 6–8 weeks
- **SNRIs** (venlafaxine, duloxetine): Useful when depression is accompanied by anxiety or pain
- **Mirtazapine**: Useful when insomnia and appetite loss are prominent
- **Bupropion**: Activating — helpful for depression with fatigue and low motivation; also used for smoking cessation

Antidepressants are typically continued for 6–12 months after full remission to prevent relapse. Stopping abruptly is not recommended.

### When to Seek Urgent Help

If you or someone you know is experiencing thoughts of suicide or self-harm, seek help immediately:

- Shifa Emergency Department: open 24/7
- Umang helpline (mental health): 0311-7786264
- Rozan Counselling Helpline: 051-888-7980

Do not leave a suicidal person alone. Remove access to means (medications, sharp objects). Stay calm and listen without judgment.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Is depression a real illness or just a state of mind?**
Depression is a real medical illness with measurable biological changes in the brain. Brain imaging studies show structural and functional differences in the depressed brain. It cannot be overcome purely by "thinking positively" or "being stronger" — it requires treatment just as any other medical condition does.

**Q: How do I know if I need medication or therapy?**
Mild to moderate depression often responds well to therapy alone. Moderate to severe depression — particularly when accompanied by inability to function, severe insomnia, appetite changes, or suicidal thinking — typically requires medication in addition to therapy. A psychiatrist can help determine the right approach based on your symptoms.

**Q: How long does depression treatment take?**
Most people begin feeling better within 4–8 weeks of starting treatment. Full remission often takes 3–6 months. Treatment is typically continued for 6–12 months after remission to prevent relapse. Some people with recurrent depression benefit from long-term maintenance treatment.

**Q: Can untreated depression get better on its own?**
Some episodes of depression do remit without treatment, but this can take months or years. Untreated depression increases the risk of recurrence, worsens physical health conditions, damages relationships and careers, and significantly increases suicide risk. Early treatment leads to faster and more complete recovery.

**Q: Is it shameful to see a psychiatrist in Pakistan?**
Stigma around mental health remains a significant barrier to treatment in Pakistan. However, attitudes are changing rapidly, particularly among younger generations and in urban areas. A psychiatrist is a medical doctor specialising in brain health — consulting one is no different from seeing a cardiologist for a heart problem. Mental health is health.

# 10 Foods That Lower Blood Pressure Naturally (Evidence-Based)

Hypertension affects approximately one-third of Pakistani adults and is a leading driver of heart attack, stroke, and kidney failure. While medication is often necessary, diet is one of the most powerful tools for reducing blood pressure — and the evidence is clear about which foods make the biggest difference.

The target: less than 130/80 mmHg for most adults; less than 140/90 mmHg if you are over 65 with significant other conditions.

## The Science Behind Diet and Blood Pressure

Blood pressure is influenced by:

- **Sodium**: The primary dietary driver — sodium causes the kidneys to retain water, increasing blood volume and pressure. The average Pakistani diet contains 9–12g of sodium per day; the target is under 5g
- **Potassium, calcium, and magnesium**: These minerals counteract sodium's effect, relax blood vessel walls, and reduce vascular resistance
- **Nitrates**: Convert to nitric oxide, which dilates blood vessels
- **Flavonoids and polyphenols**: Improve endothelial function (the health of the inner lining of blood vessels)

## 10 Foods That Lower Blood Pressure

### 1. Leafy Green Vegetables (Spinach, Kale, Palak)
Rich in potassium and nitrates. Potassium helps the kidneys excrete more sodium in urine. One cup of cooked spinach provides approximately 840 mg of potassium — 18% of the daily recommended intake. Aim for 2–3 servings daily.

**Pakistani use**: Palak gosht, saag, spinach daal, or simply add fresh spinach leaves to roti toppings or egg dishes.

### 2. Berries (Blueberries, Strawberries, Pomegranate)
High in anthocyanins — flavonoids that improve arterial flexibility and reduce the stiffness associated with high blood pressure. A study in the American Journal of Clinical Nutrition found that people who consumed the most anthocyanins had 8% lower risk of hypertension.

**Pakistani use**: Fresh pomegranate as a snack, strawberry yoghurt (no sugar added), or fresh berries when in season.

### 3. Beetroot
Exceptionally high in inorganic nitrates. Studies show beetroot juice can reduce systolic blood pressure by 4–10 mmHg within hours of consumption. The effect is sustained with daily consumption.

**Pakistani use**: Add raw beetroot to salads, blend into fresh juice, or roast with minimal oil.

### 4. Oats
Beta-glucan — a soluble fibre in oats — reduces both systolic and diastolic blood pressure. A meta-analysis of 28 randomised trials found oat consumption reduced systolic blood pressure by 2.7 mmHg on average. This fibre also reduces LDL cholesterol.

**Pakistani use**: Replace white bread with oats for breakfast; oats with fruit and nuts (no added sugar).

### 5. Bananas
One of the most potassium-dense commonly available fruits in Pakistan. One medium banana provides approximately 422 mg of potassium. Daily banana consumption significantly reduces the pressor effect of sodium.

**Pakistani use**: Eaten fresh, added to dahi (yoghurt), or sliced over oats.

### 6. Low-Fat Dairy (Dahi, Milk)
Rich in calcium and bioactive peptides that reduce ACE activity (the same mechanism as ACE-inhibitor medications). The DASH diet — the most evidence-supported dietary pattern for blood pressure — includes 2–3 daily servings of low-fat dairy.

**Pakistani use**: Unsweetened dahi (plain yoghurt) with meals. Choose low-fat over full-fat versions. Avoid sweet lassi.

### 7. Fatty Fish (Salmon, Sardines, Mackerel — Rohu, Singhara)
Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA and DHA) reduce blood pressure by improving endothelial function, reducing inflammation, and slightly reducing heart rate. A meta-analysis found omega-3 supplementation reduced systolic blood pressure by 4.5 mmHg in hypertensive individuals.

**Pakistani use**: Freshwater fish (rohu, singhara) grilled or baked 2–3 times per week. Limit frying, which adds unhealthy fats.

### 8. Garlic
Allicin — the active compound in garlic — acts as a natural ACE inhibitor and calcium channel blocker. Meta-analyses show garlic supplementation reduces systolic blood pressure by approximately 5 mmHg in hypertensive individuals.

**Pakistani use**: Already a staple in Pakistani cooking. For maximum benefit, crush garlic and let it sit for 10 minutes before cooking to allow allicin to form.

### 9. Lentils and Legumes (Daal, Masoor, Chana)
High in potassium, magnesium, and protein — all beneficial for blood pressure. A dietary pattern high in legumes is independently associated with lower blood pressure. They also replace higher-sodium meat proteins in the diet.

**Pakistani use**: Daal is already central to Pakistani diets. Choose minimally processed preparations with reduced added salt and ghee.

### 10. Dark Chocolate (70%+ Cocoa)
Flavanols in dark chocolate improve nitric oxide production, dilating blood vessels. Meta-analyses show dark chocolate reduces systolic blood pressure by approximately 2–3 mmHg. Effects are seen with as little as 6–10 g (one small square) daily.

**Pakistani use**: Choose dark chocolate (70% cocoa or higher) rather than milk chocolate. Avoid chocolate bars with added milk and sugar.

## Foods to Reduce

While adding beneficial foods, reducing these has equal or greater impact:

- **Table salt and salty condiments**: Replace with herbs and spices (cumin, coriander, turmeric) rather than salt
- **Processed and packaged foods**: Chips, biscuits, pickles, sauces — often high in hidden sodium
- **Fried foods**: High in trans and saturated fats that damage blood vessels
- **Sweetened beverages**: Increase insulin resistance and inflammation
- **Excess red meat**: Associated with higher blood pressure compared to plant and fish protein

## The DASH Diet Pattern

The Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension (DASH) diet is the most well-researched dietary pattern for blood pressure reduction:

- 8–10 servings of fruits and vegetables daily
- 2–3 servings of low-fat dairy
- Whole grains as the primary carbohydrate source
- Lean protein (fish, poultry, legumes)
- Nuts and seeds in small quantities
- Sodium target: under 1,500–2,300 mg/day

Following DASH faithfully reduces systolic blood pressure by 8–14 mmHg — equivalent to one antihypertensive medication.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can diet alone control high blood pressure without medication?**
For mild to moderate hypertension (systolic 130–159 mmHg), lifestyle changes including diet, exercise, and weight loss can normalise blood pressure without medication in many people. For moderate-to-severe hypertension, medication is usually needed alongside dietary changes. Never stop prescribed antihypertensive medication without consulting your doctor.

**Q: How much sodium is in Pakistani food?**
Pakistani cuisine is among the highest-sodium diets globally. A typical Pakistani meal of curry, rice, and roti can contain 1,500–3,000 mg of sodium — the entire daily recommended allowance in one meal. Key culprits: soy sauce, store-bought masalas, biryani spice mixes, achaar (pickles), and processed meats.

**Q: How quickly can diet changes lower blood pressure?**
Significant reductions can appear within 2–4 weeks of consistent dietary change. Studies show the DASH diet produces measurable reductions within two weeks. The full effect of weight loss on blood pressure takes longer — often 3–6 months.

**Q: Is coconut water good for blood pressure?**
Coconut water is high in potassium (~600 mg per cup) and has been shown in small studies to reduce blood pressure modestly. It is a healthier choice than sugary beverages, but should not replace consistent dietary and lifestyle management.

**Q: Does green tea lower blood pressure?**
Catechins in green tea improve endothelial function and modestly reduce blood pressure (by approximately 1–2 mmHg in regular drinkers). Benefits are modest and green tea should not replace established dietary strategies.

# Understanding Your Blood Test Results: A Complete Guide

Receiving a lab report full of numbers and acronyms can be confusing or alarming — particularly when values are flagged as "H" (high) or "L" (low). This guide explains the most common blood tests, what each value measures, and what abnormal results may mean.

Important: this guide is for education only. Abnormal results must always be interpreted by your doctor in the context of your symptoms, medications, and medical history.

## Complete Blood Count (CBC)

The CBC measures the three types of cells in your blood: red cells, white cells, and platelets.

### Red Blood Cell Parameters

**Haemoglobin (Hb)**
Measures the oxygen-carrying protein in red blood cells.
- Normal: Men 13.5–17.5 g/dL; Women 12.0–15.5 g/dL
- Low (anaemia): Fatigue, breathlessness, pallor. Causes include iron deficiency, vitamin B12/folate deficiency, chronic disease, blood loss, thalassaemia
- High (polycythaemia): Can indicate dehydration, lung disease, or a blood disorder

**MCV (Mean Corpuscular Volume)**
Measures the average size of red blood cells.
- Normal: 80–100 fL
- Low MCV (small red cells): Iron deficiency anaemia, thalassaemia trait
- High MCV (large red cells): B12 or folate deficiency; alcohol excess; certain medications

**Ferritin**
Measures stored iron in the body. Low ferritin confirms iron deficiency even when haemoglobin is still normal. Normal: Men 30–300 ng/mL; Women 15–200 ng/mL.

### White Blood Cell (WBC) Count

**Total WBC**: 4.0–11.0 × 10⁹/L
- High (leucocytosis): Bacterial infection, inflammation, stress, steroids, leukaemia
- Low (leucopaenia): Viral infections, autoimmune disease, bone marrow suppression, certain medications

**Neutrophils** (50–70% of WBC): Fight bacterial infections. Elevated in bacterial infection, steroids. Low in viral infections and chemotherapy.

**Lymphocytes** (20–40%): Fight viral infections. Elevated in viral illness, chronic lymphocytic leukaemia. Low in HIV, immunosuppression.

**Eosinophils** (1–4%): Elevated in allergic conditions and parasitic infections — very relevant in Pakistan.

### Platelets (150–400 × 10⁹/L)
- Low (thrombocytopaenia): Risk of bleeding. Causes: dengue fever (very common in Pakistan), viral illness, immune thrombocytopaenia (ITP), certain medications
- High (thrombocytosis): Can indicate inflammation, iron deficiency, or a myeloproliferative disorder

## Lipid Panel

### Total Cholesterol
- Desirable: < 5.2 mmol/L (< 200 mg/dL)
- Borderline high: 5.2–6.2 mmol/L (200–239 mg/dL)
- High: ≥ 6.2 mmol/L (≥ 240 mg/dL)

### LDL Cholesterol ("Bad" Cholesterol)
Targets vary by cardiovascular risk:
- Low risk: < 3.0 mmol/L (< 116 mg/dL)
- Moderate risk: < 2.6 mmol/L (< 100 mg/dL)
- High risk (diabetes, heart disease, prior stroke): < 1.8 mmol/L (< 70 mg/dL)

### HDL Cholesterol ("Good" Cholesterol)
- Desirable: ≥ 1.0 mmol/L (≥ 40 mg/dL) for men; ≥ 1.3 mmol/L (≥ 50 mg/dL) for women
- Low HDL increases cardiovascular risk independent of LDL

### Triglycerides
- Normal: < 1.7 mmol/L (< 150 mg/dL)
- Elevated in: poor diet, obesity, diabetes, alcohol use, hypothyroidism, certain medications

## Liver Function Tests (LFTs)

**ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase)**: The most liver-specific enzyme.
- Normal: 7–56 U/L (varies by laboratory)
- Elevated ALT: Indicates hepatocyte damage — hepatitis B or C, fatty liver disease (NAFLD), alcohol, medications

**AST (Aspartate Aminotransferase)**
- Normal: 10–40 U/L
- Elevated in liver disease, but also in muscle damage and heart attack. An AST:ALT ratio > 2:1 suggests alcoholic liver disease.

**ALP (Alkaline Phosphatase)**
- Normal: 44–147 U/L
- Elevated in: cholestatic liver disease (bile duct blockage), bone disease, pregnancy

**Bilirubin** (Total)
- Normal: 3–20 µmol/L (0.1–1.2 mg/dL)
- Elevated: Jaundice. Causes include haemolysis, hepatitis, bile duct obstruction, and Gilbert's syndrome (a benign genetic variant very common in Pakistan)

**Albumin**
- Normal: 35–50 g/L
- Low albumin: A sign of chronic liver disease, malnutrition, or significant inflammation

## Kidney Function Tests (RFTs)

**Creatinine**
- Normal: Men 62–115 µmol/L (0.7–1.3 mg/dL); Women 53–97 µmol/L (0.6–1.1 mg/dL)
- Elevated creatinine indicates reduced kidney function. Muscle mass affects creatinine levels — athletes may have higher creatinine within normal kidney function.

**eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate)**
The most useful kidney function measure. Calculated from creatinine, age, and sex.
- Normal: ≥ 90 mL/min/1.73m²
- Stages of CKD: G1 (≥90, normal/high), G2 (60–89), G3a (45–59), G3b (30–44), G4 (15–29), G5 (<15, kidney failure)

**Urea (BUN)**
- Normal: 2.5–7.1 mmol/L (7–20 mg/dL)
- Elevated in dehydration, high protein intake, kidney disease, GI bleeding

**Urine Microalbumin-to-Creatinine Ratio (UACR)**
- Normal: < 3 mg/mmol (< 30 mg/g)
- Elevated UACR (microalbuminuria): Early kidney damage — especially important in diabetics and hypertensives who need annual testing

## Thyroid Function Tests

**TSH (Thyroid Stimulating Hormone)**
The most sensitive single test for thyroid function.
- Normal: 0.4–4.0 mIU/L
- High TSH: Hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid) — fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, constipation
- Low TSH: Hyperthyroidism (overactive thyroid) — weight loss, palpitations, heat intolerance, anxiety

**Free T4 (fT4)**
- Normal: 12–22 pmol/L
- Low fT4 with high TSH: Primary hypothyroidism (e.g., Hashimoto's thyroiditis)
- High fT4 with low TSH: Primary hyperthyroidism (e.g., Graves' disease)

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: My result is flagged "H" or "L" — should I be worried?**
Not necessarily. Reference ranges are defined as the range covering 95% of the healthy population — meaning 5% of healthy people have values outside the normal range. A single mildly abnormal result in the absence of symptoms often requires only repeat testing. Your doctor interprets results in context of your whole clinical picture.

**Q: Why do different labs have different normal ranges?**
Reference ranges depend on the specific analyser, reagent, and calibration used by each laboratory — as well as the population studied during method validation. Always use the reference range printed on your own lab report, not values from online references.

**Q: Do I need to fast before blood tests?**
Fasting is required for: lipid panel, fasting blood glucose, and fasting insulin. Fasting is not required for: CBC, kidney function tests, thyroid tests, and most other routine tests. When in doubt, check with the ordering physician.

**Q: Can medications affect lab results?**
Yes. Many common medications affect lab values: steroids raise blood glucose and WBC; statins can mildly raise liver enzymes; diuretics affect electrolytes; biotin supplements can interfere with thyroid and hormone tests. Always inform your laboratory and doctor about all medications and supplements.

**Q: How often should routine blood tests be done?**
For healthy adults with no risk factors: a comprehensive metabolic panel and CBC every 2–3 years in their 20s–30s; annually from age 40 onwards. People with diabetes, hypertension, or kidney disease need more frequent monitoring — typically every 3–6 months. Your doctor will determine the right schedule for you.

# Common Health Problems in Older Adults: Prevention and Management

Pakistan's population is ageing: adults over 60 will represent 12% of the population by 2050, up from approximately 7% today. Age brings with it a higher risk of certain conditions — but most are preventable, all are manageable, and none are inevitable consequences of ageing.

## 1. Falls and Fall-Related Injuries

Falls are the leading cause of injury-related death and disability in adults over 65. In Pakistan, fractures from falls (particularly hip fractures) carry a high mortality rate in older adults due to complications of immobility.

**Risk factors**: Poor vision, muscle weakness, balance impairment, medications that cause dizziness (antihypertensives, sedatives), low blood pressure on standing (orthostatic hypotension), cognitive impairment, home hazards (loose rugs, poor lighting, slippery bathroom floors).

**Prevention**:
- Annual vision check and updated corrective lenses
- Vitamin D supplementation (800–1,000 IU/day) — deficiency is near-universal in older Pakistani adults and significantly reduces balance and muscle strength
- Resistance training and balance exercises (Tai Chi has the strongest evidence for falls reduction in older adults)
- Medication review — identify and reduce medications that cause dizziness or sedation
- Home hazard assessment: non-slip mats in the bathroom, grab bars, adequate night lighting
- Calcium intake 1,000–1,200 mg/day (dietary first, supplement if needed)

## 2. Osteoporosis

Osteoporosis — thinning and weakening of bones — affects 1 in 3 women and 1 in 5 men over 50. It is largely silent until a fracture occurs.

**Signs**: No symptoms until fracture. Back pain from vertebral fractures. Loss of height. Stooped posture.

**Screening**: DEXA bone density scan (dual-energy X-ray absorptiometry) recommended for women over 65, postmenopausal women with risk factors, and men over 70 or with significant risk factors.

**Treatment**: Calcium, vitamin D, weight-bearing exercise, fall prevention, and bisphosphonate medications (alendronate, risedronate, zoledronic acid) for those with confirmed osteoporosis or high fracture risk.

## 3. Cognitive Decline and Dementia

Dementia is not a normal part of ageing — it is a disease. Alzheimer's disease is the most common type (60–70%), followed by vascular dementia (often related to strokes and blood vessel damage).

**Early signs**: Repeating questions or stories; getting lost in familiar places; difficulty managing finances or medications; personality changes; word-finding difficulties; forgetting appointments.

**What normal ageing looks like**: Occasionally forgetting a name but remembering it later; slower processing speed; some difficulty with multitasking. These are not dementia.

**Risk reduction**: Control blood pressure, blood glucose, and cholesterol from middle age. Stay socially and intellectually active. Exercise regularly. Treat hearing loss (a significant modifiable risk factor). Avoid smoking.

**Management**: Medication (donepezil, rivastigmine) modestly improves symptoms in Alzheimer's; regular structured activity, caregiver support, and a safe home environment are essential.

## 4. Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)

Hypertension affects over 50% of adults over 60 in Pakistan. Risk rises with age as arteries stiffen — but this is not inevitable. Uncontrolled blood pressure in older adults dramatically increases risk of stroke, heart failure, and kidney failure.

**Target**: The 2023 ESC guidelines recommend a systolic target of 120–130 mmHg in adults under 70 who tolerate treatment well; up to 140 mmHg for those over 70 with frailty or significant comorbidities.

**Caution in older adults**: Starting antihypertensive medications too aggressively in frail elderly patients can cause orthostatic hypotension and falls. Begin at lower doses and titrate slowly.

## 5. Type 2 Diabetes

Prevalence of diabetes increases sharply with age, peaking in adults over 65. Older adults with diabetes have higher rates of falls, cognitive decline, depression, and functional decline — making glucose control one component of a broader management strategy.

**Special considerations in older adults**:
- HbA1c targets are less strict (< 8.0–8.5%) to avoid hypoglycaemia, which in older adults can cause falls and cardiac events
- Hypoglycaemia in older adults can present atypically — with confusion, diaphoresis, or falls rather than tremor and palpitations
- Simplified medication regimens (once-daily dosing) improve adherence
- Regular review of polypharmacy — older diabetics are often on 6 or more medications

## 6. Heart Failure

Heart failure — in which the heart does not pump efficiently — becomes increasingly common with age, particularly after heart attack, longstanding hypertension, or valvular heart disease.

**Symptoms**: Breathlessness on exertion (progressing to at rest), leg swelling (pitting oedema), reduced exercise tolerance, orthopnoea (breathlessness when lying flat), nocturnal cough.

**Red flags requiring urgent evaluation**: Sudden worsening breathlessness, chest pain, weight gain of > 2 kg in 2 days, inability to complete a sentence.

**Management**: Medications (ACE inhibitors/ARBs, beta blockers, SGLT-2 inhibitors, diuretics), fluid and salt restriction, daily weight monitoring, structured cardiac rehabilitation.

## 7. Depression and Loneliness

Depression is underdiagnosed in older adults because symptoms are often attributed to "normal ageing" or dismissed as physical complaints. Social isolation — exacerbated by loss of a spouse, reduced mobility, or retirement — is an independent risk factor for depression, dementia, and premature death.

**Signs of depression in older adults**: Withdrawal from social activities, loss of interest in hobbies, changes in sleep or appetite, unexplained somatic complaints, hopelessness.

**Treatment**: Equally effective as in younger adults — antidepressants and psychotherapy. Ensure adequate social engagement, structured daily activity, and family involvement in care.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: At what age should someone start seeing a geriatrician?**
There is no fixed age threshold. Geriatric assessment is beneficial when a patient has multiple chronic conditions being managed by several specialists (polypharmacy risk), when frailty or functional decline is present, when falls or cognitive concerns arise, or when coordinating complex care becomes difficult. Most geriatricians in Pakistan see patients from age 65–70 onwards.

**Q: How much physical activity is safe for older adults?**
The WHO recommends 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity activity for adults over 65, plus muscle-strengthening exercises 2 days per week. For frail adults, any level of movement — even from sitting — provides benefit. Exercise is one of the most powerful interventions available for preventing falls, dementia, depression, and functional decline.

**Q: Is memory loss always a sign of dementia?**
No. Memory loss has many causes: sleep deprivation, vitamin B12 deficiency, hypothyroidism, depression, medication side effects (particularly anticholinergic drugs), and normal ageing. These are all treatable. A proper evaluation by a doctor is needed before any conclusion about dementia.

**Q: What vaccines do older adults need?**
Adults over 65 should receive: annual influenza vaccine, pneumococcal vaccines (PCV15/PCV20 or PPSV23), COVID-19 vaccine and boosters, and Td booster every 10 years (or Tdap once for pertussis protection). Herpes zoster (shingles) vaccine (Shingrix) is recommended from age 50 but not yet widely available in Pakistan.

**Q: How can families support an elderly relative with multiple health conditions?**
Create a single medication list and bring it to every appointment. Help with transportation to medical appointments. Monitor for falls, weight changes, and mood changes. Communicate directly with healthcare providers when the patient has difficulty expressing themselves. Explore home care services when daily activities become unsafe without assistance.

# Heart Attack First Aid: Step-by-Step Guide

If someone near you may be having a heart attack, the next few minutes matter enormously. You do not need medical training to make a life-saving difference. Follow these steps.

## Recognise the Warning Signs

Before acting, confirm you are likely dealing with a cardiac emergency:

- Chest pain, pressure, or tightness (the person may describe it as an elephant sitting on their chest)
- Pain spreading to one or both arms, the jaw, neck, or back
- Sudden shortness of breath
- Cold sweat, nausea, or lightheadedness
- Pale, ashen, or grey skin colour

Note the exact time symptoms began — emergency staff will need this.

## Step 1: Call Emergency Services (1122 or 115) Immediately

Do not wait to see if symptoms improve. Do not drive the person to hospital yourself unless you are trained to do so and the ambulance wait time is very long with no alternative.

Tell the operator:
- "I believe someone is having a heart attack."
- The person's approximate age
- The exact address or nearby landmark
- Whether the person is conscious and breathing

Stay on the line. The operator will guide you.

## Step 2: Have the Person Sit or Lie Down

Help them into a comfortable position — usually sitting upright against a wall or lying with their head and shoulders slightly raised. This reduces the strain on the heart compared to standing.

Loosen tight clothing: collar buttons, ties, belts. This aids breathing.

Do not let them walk around or exert themselves.

## Step 3: Give Aspirin (If Available and Appropriate)

If the person:
- Is conscious and able to swallow
- Is not known to be allergic to aspirin
- Has not been told by a doctor never to take aspirin
- Has no signs of a stomach bleed

Then give 300mg aspirin (e.g., one adult aspirin tablet or three 100mg tablets). Ask them to chew it rather than swallow whole — chewing achieves faster blood absorption.

Do not give aspirin to a person who is unconscious, allergic, or if you are unsure.

## Step 4: Keep Them Calm and Monitored

Reassure the person. Anxiety increases the heart's oxygen demand and can worsen the situation. Tell them clearly: "Emergency services are on the way. You are not alone. Stay calm and breathe slowly."

Monitor: Are they conscious? Are they breathing? Are symptoms worsening?

Unlock or open the front door so paramedics can enter immediately.

## Step 5: If They Lose Consciousness and Stop Breathing Normally — Begin CPR

If the person becomes unresponsive and is not breathing normally (normal breathing means regular breaths — occasional gasps are NOT normal breathing), they have likely gone into cardiac arrest.

### How to Perform Hands-Only CPR

1. Place the heel of one hand on the centre of the chest (on the breastbone)
2. Place your second hand on top, fingers interlaced
3. Keep arms straight. Press down firmly — 5–6 cm (about 2 inches)
4. Release fully, allowing the chest to rise
5. Repeat at a rate of 100–120 compressions per minute (the rhythm of the song "Stayin' Alive" by Bee Gees works as a tempo guide)
6. Continue without stopping until emergency services arrive or the person regains consciousness

You do not need to give rescue breaths (mouth-to-mouth) unless you are trained. Hands-only CPR is effective and recommended for untrained bystanders.

### Using an AED (Automated External Defibrillator)

If an AED is available nearby (in airports, shopping centres, and some offices):
1. Turn it on — it will provide clear voice instructions
2. Attach pads to bare skin as shown on the diagram
3. Follow all voice instructions
4. Continue CPR between AED analyses

AEDs are designed for untrained bystanders — they will not shock unless a shockable rhythm is detected.

## What NOT to Do

- Do not give food or water
- Do not give medication other than aspirin (unless prescribed, e.g., nitroglycerin)
- Do not leave the person alone
- Do not let them "walk it off" or insist it is indigestion
- Do not assume it is too early or not serious enough to call emergency services

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How do I know if CPR is working?**
In cardiac arrest, the goal of CPR is to maintain blood flow to the brain and vital organs until a defibrillator or paramedics can restore a normal heart rhythm. You will not see the person wake up during CPR in most cases — keep going. You are buying time, not curing the arrest.

**Q: Can I hurt someone by doing CPR incorrectly?**
Rib fractures are a common complication of CPR — this is expected and acceptable. A person in cardiac arrest will die without CPR. The risk of rib fractures from CPR is far outweighed by the benefit. Start CPR if you have any reason to believe the person is in cardiac arrest and do not stop until emergency services take over.

**Q: How long should I continue CPR?**
Continue until: emergency services arrive and take over, an AED advises you to stop, or you are physically unable to continue. Do not stop based on time alone.

**Q: What if I am not sure it is a heart attack?**
When in doubt, call emergency services. It is always better to call and not need emergency care than to delay calling when it was needed. Emergency operators are trained to help you assess the situation.

# When Is a Headache Serious? Red Flags and When to Seek Urgent Care

Headache is one of the most common symptoms worldwide — and in the vast majority of cases, it is not dangerous. However, certain headache characteristics signal a potentially life-threatening condition that requires emergency evaluation. Knowing the difference can save your life or that of someone you know.

## The Three Most Common Headache Types

### Tension-Type Headache
The most common headache type. Presents as a dull, pressing, or tightening pain — often described as a tight band around the head. Bilateral (both sides). Mild to moderate intensity. Not worsened by routine activity. No nausea. No visual symptoms.

Triggers: stress, poor posture, muscle tension in the neck and shoulders, dehydration, irregular sleep, eye strain.

Treatment: paracetamol or ibuprofen, hydration, rest, physical therapy for chronic cases.

### Migraine
A neurological condition — not simply a "bad headache." Migraine affects approximately 15% of the population and is the third most prevalent disease worldwide.

Classic features:
- Moderate to severe, throbbing or pulsating pain
- Unilateral (one side) in most cases
- Worsened by routine physical activity
- Accompanied by nausea, vomiting, and/or sensitivity to light (photophobia) and sound (phonophobia)
- Duration 4–72 hours if untreated

Approximately 25–30% of migraineurs experience an **aura** — reversible neurological symptoms (usually visual — zigzag lines, flickering lights, or blind spots) that precede the headache by 20–60 minutes.

Treatment: triptans (sumatriptan) for acute attacks; preventive medications (propranolol, amitriptyline, topiramate, CGRP inhibitors) for frequent migraine.

### Cluster Headache
Rare but extremely severe. Excruciating, unilateral pain around or behind the eye. Lasts 15–180 minutes but occurs in "clusters" — multiple times per day for weeks or months, then remits. Associated with eye redness, tearing, nasal congestion, and agitation (patients cannot stay still, unlike migraine patients who prefer to lie still).

Predominantly affects men. Treated acutely with 100% oxygen and sumatriptan injection; preventively with verapamil.

## Red Flag Headaches: Seek Emergency Care Immediately

Use the **SNNOOP10 mnemonic** to identify red flags:

- **S** — Systemic symptoms (fever, neck stiffness, rash)
- **N** — Neurological symptoms (weakness, numbness, speech change, confusion, vision loss)
- **N** — New headache onset after age 50
- **O** — Onset sudden (thunderclap headache — see below)
- **O** — Older: first severe headache after age 65
- **P** — Positional: headache that worsens significantly lying down or with Valsalva (coughing, straining)
- **P** — Papilloedema (blurred disc margins on eye examination — indicates raised intracranial pressure)
- **P** — Progressive pattern — worsening over weeks
- **P** — Preceding headache that is changing in character
- **P** — Pregnancy or post-partum
- **10** — Trauma preceding the headache (head injury)

### The Thunderclap Headache

**The most important headache red flag.** A headache that reaches maximum severity within 60 seconds — described as "the worst headache of my life" or "like being hit on the head with a bat" — is a subarachnoid haemorrhage (bleeding around the brain) until proven otherwise.

Subarachnoid haemorrhage is a potentially fatal emergency. Caused most commonly by rupture of a cerebral aneurysm. Survival depends entirely on how quickly the person reaches a neurosurgical centre.

If you or someone near you experiences a sudden severe headache unlike any previous headache, call emergency services immediately.

### Headache with Fever and Neck Stiffness

This triad — headache, fever, and neck stiffness — is the classic presentation of bacterial meningitis. Rash (non-blanching purple-red spots — petechiae or purpura) confirms meningococcal disease.

Bacterial meningitis is a life-threatening emergency. Treatment delay worsens outcomes dramatically. Do not wait to see if fever resolves.

### Headache After Head Injury

Post-traumatic headache is common and usually benign. However, a headache that develops hours or days after a head injury — particularly with drowsiness, confusion, repeated vomiting, or weakness — may indicate a subdural or epidural haematoma (blood accumulating between the skull and brain). This requires urgent imaging.

## When to Book a Non-Urgent Appointment

See your doctor for non-emergency evaluation when:

- Headaches are frequent (more than 15 days per month)
- Headaches are progressively worsening over weeks
- Over-the-counter medications are no longer effective
- Headaches consistently wake you from sleep
- You are using pain medication more than 10–15 days per month (medication overuse headache)
- A new type of headache develops that is different from your usual pattern

## Medication Overuse Headache

A common but underrecognised cause of chronic daily headache. Taking analgesics (paracetamol, ibuprofen, aspirin, triptans) on more than 10–15 days per month causes the headache to "rebound" and become chronic. The treatment — counterintuitively — is withdrawal of the overused medication, which initially worsens headache before improvement occurs. This should be done under medical supervision.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: How do I know if my headache is a migraine or a tension headache?**
The key differences: migraine is typically unilateral and throbbing, accompanied by nausea and light/sound sensitivity, and worsened by movement. Tension headache is bilateral, pressing or tightening, mild to moderate, without nausea or significant light sensitivity, and not worsened by normal activity.

**Q: What causes headaches every day?**
Chronic daily headache (headache on 15 or more days per month) has several common causes: medication overuse, chronic migraine, chronic tension-type headache, sleep disorders, anxiety and depression, and (rarely) structural causes. A neurology evaluation is recommended to identify and treat the underlying cause.

**Q: Are headaches behind the eyes always serious?**
Pain behind or around the eye is common in migraine, cluster headache, sinusitis, and eye strain. It is serious when it is sudden and severe (possible aneurysm), when accompanied by vision loss or double vision (possible optic neuritis or third nerve palsy), or when accompanied by a red, painful eye and halos (possible acute angle-closure glaucoma — an ocular emergency).

**Q: Can dehydration cause severe headache?**
Dehydration is a common headache trigger — usually producing a dull, throbbing bilateral headache that improves with rehydration over 1–3 hours. Dehydration does not cause thunderclap headache or headache with neurological symptoms. If a headache is severe and does not improve with fluids and paracetamol, do not attribute it to dehydration.

**Q: When should I go to emergency for a headache?**
Go immediately to the emergency department for: sudden severe headache (worst of your life), headache with fever and neck stiffness, headache with weakness/numbness/speech difficulty, headache after head injury, headache with vision changes, and any headache in a person with HIV/AIDS or immunosuppression.
